[49] INTRODUCTION
[50] 1.1 Background Information
[51] University course timetabling is the process of assigning courses, lecturers, rooms, and time slots in a way that meets all required rules and respects preferences as much as possible. The required rules (hard constraints) include no lecturer teaching two classes at the same time, no student group having two classes in the same slot, and rooms being large enough for the number of students. The preferred rules (soft constraints) include lecturers teaching at their chosen times, avoiding long gaps between classes for students, and keeping related courses on the same day where possible courses and constraints increase (Babaei, Karimpour, & Hadidi, 2015; Chen, Sze, Goh, Sabar, & Kendall, 2021a). Because of this difficulty, researchers and developers use heuristic and meta-heuristic methods that produce good-quality timetables within acceptable time limits. Common methods include genetic algorithms, simulated annealing, and tabu search. These approaches have been applied successfully to real university datasets and have reduced clashes and impr(Lewis & Thompson, 2015; Pillay, 2016a). As universities have grown in size and complexity, with more courses, part-time lecturers, shared facilities, and hybrid teaching modes, manual methods have become inadequate.
[52] The introduction of computer-based systems has significantly improved the process. University course timetabling is recognized as an NP-hard optimization problem, which means that finding the best possible solution becomes extremely difficult as the number of courses and constraints increases (Babaei et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2021a). Because of this difficulty, researchers and developers use heuristic and meta-heuristic methods that produce good-quality timetables within acceptable time limits. Common methods include genetic algorithms, simulated annealing, and tabu search. These approaches have been applied successfully to real university datasets and have reduced clashes and improved room utilization compared with manual schedules (Al-Betar, Khader, & Zaman, 2012; Lü & Hao, n.d.).
[53] However, most existing timetabling systems require input data to be provided in structured formats such as spreadsheets or database files. This requirement creates a barrier for non-technical users, including departmental administrators and academic planning officers who may not have the necessary skills to prepare the data correctly. Recent advances in natural language processing (NLP) offer a solution to this problem. NLP techniques enable systems to accept requirements written in ordinary English and automatically extract the relevant courses, lecturers, rooms, and constraints (Chen et al., 2021a; Milani Fitria, 2023). Studies conducted between 2021 and 2024 have demonstrated that NLP-based input reduces preparation time and makes scheduling tools accessible to staff without programming knowledge(Nguyen, Le, Hoang, & Nguyen, 2021).
[54] Hyper-heuristics provide an additional improvement by managing several low-level heuristics (such as genetic algorithms, simulated annealing, and tabu search) and selecting or combining them during the search process. This approach increases flexibility and often produces better results than using a single method for all instances(Ahmed, Enderözcan, & Kheiri, n.d.; Drake, Kheiri, Özcan, & Burke, 2020). Recent work has shown that hyper-heuristics perform well on standard timetabling benchmarks and on real university data.
[55] The present study builds on these developments by creating a system that accepts natural language input, converts it into formal constraints using current NLP methods, and then applies multiple hyper-heuristic algorithms (genetic algorithm, simulated annealing, and tabu search) to generate timetables. The system will compare the performance of the three algorithms on the same set of constraints and display the results to the user. This combination addresses the need for an accessible, effective, and comparative timetabling tool suitable for use in Covenant University and similar institutions.
[56] 1.2 Statement of the Problem
[57] Most existing university timetabling systems require users to enter data in fixed formats, such as Excel spreadsheets or database files. This creates a major difficulty for many school staff who handle scheduling but do not have programming or technical skills. When non-technical users try to prepare the data themselves, they often make mistakes that lead to incorrect timetables. As a result, administrators spend extra time fixing errors manually, and the final schedule still contains clashes or poor room assignments (Jaradat et al., 2019; Soria-Alcaraz et al., 2016). These problems reduce efficiency and cause frustration for both staff and students.
[58] Another important limitation is that no single optimization algorithm performs best for every type of timetable or set of constraints. For example, genetic algorithms may work well when there are many lecturer preferences, while simulated annealing might be faster when room availability is very limited. Without testing different algorithms on the actual constraints of a particular semester, it is difficult to know which one will give the best result (Ahmed et al., n.d.; Drake et al., 2020). Current systems usually offer only one fixed algorithm, so users cannot see or choose the most suitable method for their specific case.
[59] In addition, very few timetabling tools can accept requirements written in ordinary English. Recent studies have shown that natural language processing (NLP) can successfully extract courses, lecturers, rooms, and rules from plain text; most practical systems still lack this feature (Pallagani et al., 2024; Abgaryan et al., 2024). When natural language input is missing, non-technical staff remain dependent on technical experts to enter the constraints correctly.
[60] Although recent studies have examined natural language processing and hyper-heuristics separately, very few have integrated natural language reasoning directly into hyper-heuristic algorithms for university course timetabling (Pallagani et al., 2024; Drake et al., 2020). This gap prevents scheduling officers from using a single system that is both easy to operate and able to select the most effective algorithm for their specific constraints.
[61] Finally, there is limited research that combines all three elements in one system: (1) natural language input, (2) multiple hyper-heuristic algorithms running on the same problem, and (3) clear comparison of their performance (penalty scores, feasibility, and running time). Without such comparison, developers and users cannot easily understand which algorithm is most effective for different kinds of university timetabling problems (Abdipoor et al., 2023; Soria-Alcaraz et al., 2016).

[101] 2.1 Preamble
[102] The area of university timetabling has undergone tremendous changes with the development of optimization techniques and artificial intelligence. This paper reviews how timetabling problems have been addressed over time, focusing on hyper-heuristic algorithms and the increasing prominence of natural language processing. Key concepts, existing systems, and the current state of affairs are discussed in detail by drawing on recent works to identify trends and gaps. The aim here is to set the stage for this project, which integrates NLP for handling user prompts with hyper-heuristics for smart and easy timetabling by all stakeholders.
[103] 2.2 Review of University Course Timetabling Problems
[104] University course timetabling is the process of allocating lectures to time slots and rooms while satisfying hard constraints like no clashes for students or lecturers and room capacity, while minimizing the violations of soft constraints, such as lecturer preferences and compact schedules. The problem is NP-hard, and real instances often include additional institutional rules that make exact methods impractical (Abdipoor, Yaakob, Goh, & Abdullah, 2023). Surveys point out that the complexity lies in the large solution space and varying constraints across institutions, including multi-section courses or hybrid teaching modes (Dunke & Nickel, 2023; Xiang, Hu, Yu, & Wang, 2024) ).
[105] Early benchmark datasets, including the Toronto instances and later the ITC-2007 datasets, were quite widely used for comparing algorithms. Curriculum-based variants (in which the constraints are driven by student enrollments) are particularly challenging due to dynamic enrollment data, which often leads to infeasible initial solutions (Abdipoor et al., 2023). Soft constraints like minimizing gaps between classes affect user satisfaction and are considered critical for solution acceptance (Xiang et al., 2024).
[106] Recent extensions concern multi-objective formulations that balance room stability with lecturer travel distances, tested on datasets from business schools with up to 1,000 events (Dunke & Nickel, 2023). Studies on hybrid teaching-test constraints for venue flexibility introduced new constraints and showed that ignoring these increases soft constraint violations by 20–30% (Xiang et al., 2024). These surveys have emphasized the need for adaptive methods, which scale to real-time updates, such as semester changes, with the requirement to keep computational times below 10 minutes for medium-sized instances (Pillay, 2016a).
[107] 2.3 Review of Heuristic and Meta-heuristic Approaches
[108] Heuristics and meta-heuristics are the dominant approaches in timetabling solutions due to intractability. Genetic algorithms (GAs) have been popular since the 1990s and are still being refined. Implementations in recent years mostly use indirect encoding or are hybridized with local search for better solution qualities on large instances. For instance, a 2024 hybrid GA combined with memetic algorithms yielded near-optimal solutions to ITC-2007 datasets, with up to 35% reductions in soft constraint violations when compared with a standard GA (Abdipoor et al., 2023). Meanwhile, another 2021 work utilized a multi-chromosome GA to handle lecturer preferences and room features and was able to produce feasible timetables for over 800 events in less than 15 minutes.
[109] Simulated annealing gives good results, with fewer parameters to tune. From the literature, adaptive cooling schedules and neighborhood structures tailored to timetabling constraints can further reduce computation time while preserving feasibility (Kiefer, Hartl, & Schnell, 2017). Iterative improvement variants solve curriculum-based problems to near feasibility, with up to 98% feasibility on real datasets, which is a 22% reduction in penalty compared to basic SA.(Kiefer et al., 2017).
[110] Tabu search is known for efficiently escaping from the local optima, thanks to memory structures. Other recent variants also include long-term memory and frequency-based diversification, attaining competitive results on the ITC-2007 benchmarks. For instance, in 2022, an adaptive tabu search used dynamic tabu tenure and solved course timetabling with uncertain student numbers, achieving solutions with no hard violations 95% of the time(Lü & Hao, n.d.). In another more recent work, also in 2018, TS with adaptive heuristic ordering has reduced soft violations by up to 40% on exam timetabling variants compared to fixed-order-based methods.

[114] 2.4 Review of Hyper-heuristic Approaches
[115] While low-level heuristics search directly for solutions, hyper-heuristics operate at a higher level by either selecting or generating low-level heuristics. Selection hyper-heuristics have reported good performance in timetabling, mainly due to their ability to adapt during the search process (Pillay, 2016a). A 2020 review indicated that in 70% of cross-domain tests, hyper-heuristics outperform single metaheuristics owing to their generality (Drake et al., 2020).
[116] Simulated annealing (SA) gives good results with fewer parameters to tune. Adaptive cooling schedules and neighborhood structures tailored to timetabling constraints can further reduce computation time while preserving feasibility (Kiefer et al., 2017). Variants with iterative improvement solve curriculum-based problems, reaching 98% feasibility on real datasets, outperforming basic SA by 22% in terms of penalty reduction (Kiefer et al., 2017).
[117] Tabu search (TS) efficiently escapes local optima thanks to memory structures. Recent variants include long-term memory and frequency-based diversification, achieving competitive results on ITC-2007 benchmarks (Lü & Hao, n.d.). Adaptive TS methods with dynamic tenure reduce soft violations and handle uncertain student numbers effectively.
[118] 2.5 Review of Natural Language Processing in Scheduling and Optimization
[119] NLP is changing how constraints are input into optimization systems, converting casual text into structured rules. Recent systematic reviews show that rule-based NLP, employing techniques like regular expression (regex) pattern matching, remains a highly effective method for requirements formalization. These deterministic approaches provide 100% accuracy within a predefined vocabulary and avoid the computational overhead associated with deep learning models (Nguyen et al., 2022). Recent work focuses on employee and appointment scheduling but translates well to universities (Tang et al., 2024).
[120] While some advanced systems have begun exploring transformer-based models like BERT or Large Language Models (LLMs) to handle ambiguous constraints (Pallagani et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023), rule-based constraint extraction is often preferred for academic and structural prototyping. Using deterministic parsing ensures that explicit institutional rules, such as "No overlapping classes for lecturers," are translated into formal machine-readable constraints without the probabilistic errors common in generative models.
[121] 2.5 Conceptual Review (Concepts and Definitions)
[122] 2.5.1 Hyper-heuristics
[123] Hyper-heuristics are search methods that choose or create heuristics to solve a problem instead of solving it directly. Selection hyper-heuristics are the most common type for timetabling and typically include a heuristic selection mechanism and a move acceptance criterion (Drake et al., 2020; Ochoa et al., 2019).
[124] 2.5.2 Optimization and NP-Hard Problems
[125] According to predetermined goals, optimization entails selecting the best or nearly best option from a wide range of options. As a classic NP-hard optimization problem, university course scheduling cannot be solved in polynomial time by any known algorithm (Babaei et al., 2015; Pillay, 2016). Real-world scenarios with hundreds of courses, lecturers, rooms, and other rules quickly render precise methods impracticable, and even figuring out whether a feasible schedule exists (ignoring soft constraints) is NP-complete (Ceschia et al., 2023; Thepphakorn et al., 2020). Heuristic and meta-heuristic approaches predominate due to this complexity, exchanging guaranteed optimality for good solutions in a reasonable amount of time (Drake et al., 2020).
[126] 2.5.3 Natural Language Processing for Constraint Extraction
[127] To extract entities, intents, and relationships from free text, modern natural language processing (NLP) employs transformer-based models such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2019). This makes it possible to automatically translate organic prompts in scheduling, like "Dr. Adebayo cannot teach on Fridays" or "no two 300-level classes in the same room," into formal constraints (Pallagani et al., 2024; Abgaryan et al., 2024).
[128] 2.5.4 Web-based Timetabling Systems
[129] Modern web-based timetabling systems are increasingly shifting toward rapid prototyping frameworks natively integrated with Python's data science ecosystem. Frameworks like Streamlit and FastAPI allow for seamless bridging between the backend optimization engine and the frontend interface without requiring separate JavaScript architectures. These setups provide automatic API documentation, interactive data visualization, and real-time schedule adjustments within a unified ecosystem (Ceschia et al., 2023).

[137] Lecturer preferred time slots.
[138] Minimizing idle gaps between a student’s classes.
[139] Ensuring compact schedules and convenient room locations for lecturers.
[140] The timetabling problem is NP-hard, meaning the solution complexity increases exponentially with the number of courses, lecturers, and rooms involved, making manual and exact mathematical approaches inefficient for real-world cases (Abdipoor, Yaakob, Goh, & Abdullah, 2023).
[141] UCTP complexity becomes even greater in institutions offering:
[142] hybrid learning delivery (face-to-face + online),
[143] multi-section courses,

[145] 2.3 Theoretical Review (Theories, Models, and Algorithmic Foundations)
[146] Timetabling problems were originally solved using exact methods, such as integer linear programming and constraint programming. Although mathematically powerful, these approaches struggle to scale when the number of constraints increases significantly.
[147] To overcome this limitation, researchers introduced metaheuristic approaches, which search for good solutions rather than perfect solutions within reasonable time. These approaches include:
[148] Genetic Algorithms (GA)
[149] Tabu Search (TS)
[150] Simulated Annealing (SA)
[151] Ant Colony Optimization (ACO)
[152] Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO)
[153] However, no single optimization method consistently performs best across all institutions. This gave rise to hyper-heuristics, which are higher-level frameworks that select or generate heuristics dynamically depending on the current state of the solution space. This makes them:
[154] more flexible across institutions with different constraints,
[155] easier to scale to large instances,
[156] more suitable for real-time updates.
[157] Multi-objective optimization has recently been introduced to address conflicting needs—such as room stability versus lecturer travel time, especially in large universities with distributed campuses (Dunke & Nickel, 2023). Modern theoretical models now incorporate dynamic objectives reflecting real university behavior, including semester timetable changes, venue flexibility, and hybrid teaching policies (Xiang et al., 2024).
[158] The shift in theory highlights the importance of:
[159] scalability,
[160] adaptivity,
[161] computational speed,with expectations that solutions be produced under 10 minutes for medium-sized timetables (Pillay, 2016).
[162] 2.4 Empirical Review (Existing Systems, Findings & Evidence from Studies)
[163] Early research relied heavily on benchmarks, especially the Toronto and ITC-2007 datasets, to compare algorithm performance. Curriculum-based timetabling became particularly difficult due to ever-changing enrollment patterns that caused even initial timetables to become infeasible (Abdipoor et al., 2023).
[164] Across many implementations, user satisfaction with the timetable depends strongly on soft constraint performance. For example, minimizing idle gaps between classes significantly increases acceptance among lecturers and students (Xiang et al., 2024).
[165] Recent empirical studies report that
[166] Optimizing room stability reduces lecturer stress and movement (Dunke & Nickel, 2023).
[167] Hybrid-teaching models introduce new venue constraints; ignoring these results in 20–30% more soft constraint violations, showing the need for updated models (Xiang et al., 2024).
[168] Adaptive or real-time timetabling systems perform better in institutions with frequent changes, course additions, or lecturer withdrawal (Pillay, 2016).
[169] Overall, existing systems suggest that hybrid timetabling methods, combining multiple heuristics and dynamic constraint handling, provide the most balance between feasibility, solution quality, and computational speed.
[170] 2.6 Existing Works
[171] Few studies evaluate "algorithm portfolio" strategies when the constraints are derived from natural language prompts instead of pre-defined structured data. While individual metaheuristics have been thoroughly compared on common benchmarks, running multiple algorithms simultaneously to select the virtual best solver—an approach known as competitive algorithm selection—provides a more robust way to handle diverse constraint patterns extracted from text (Drake et al., 2020).
[172] Furthermore, rather than addressing the intricate multi-constraint university course scheduling problem, the majority of prompt-based scheduling tools concentrate on meeting spaces or straightforward resource allocation. Additionally, not much research has been done on giving end users comparative feedback regarding which algorithm worked best for their particular prompt (Abgaryan et al., 2024; Drake et al., 2020).
[173] By developing a comprehensive system that accepts English prompts, extracts constraints using rule-based NLP techniques, runs a portfolio of algorithms (GA, SA, and TS), and reports comparative performance, this project fills in these gaps. It provides both a useful tool and empirical insights into algorithm robustness and competitive selection under real-world language-driven inputs.

[189] 3.3 System Analysis
[190] 3.3.1 Analysis of the Existing System
[191] Traditional university timetable construction at most Nigerian institutions, including Covenant University (the institution whose academic dataset forms the basis of this study), is mostly manual or semi-automated. The existing system relies on spreadsheet-based methods where academic administrators manually assign courses to timeslots and venues. This approach suffers from several critical limitations:
[192] High Error Rate: Manual scheduling is prone to lecturer clashes (a lecturer assigned to two courses at the same time), room conflicts (two courses assigned to the same room at the same time), and venue capacity violations (students exceeding room capacity).
[193] Time Intensive: The process typically requires several weeks of iterative adjustments before producing a feasible timetable.
[194] Scalability Issues: As the number of courses, lecturers, venues, and student enrollments increases, the combinatorial complexity grows exponentially, making manual approaches impractical.
[195] Lack of Optimization: The existing system produces feasible timetables at best but does not optimize for quality metrics such as minimizing room changes, distributing classes evenly across the week, or respecting soft preferences.
[196] No Natural Language Interface: Administrators must translate institutional policies and scheduling preferences into rigid spreadsheet formats, with no mechanism for expressing constraints in natural language.
[197] 3.3.2 Analysis of the Proposed System
[198] The proposed system addresses the identified limitations through an intelligent, automated timetable generation platform with the following capabilities:
[199] Natural Language Constraint Specification: Administrators input scheduling constraints in plain English (e.g., "No lecturer should teach two classes at the same time," "Morning classes are preferred"), which are parsed by the NLP module and translated into machine-readable constraint objects.
[200] Multi-Algorithm Optimization: Three hyper-heuristic algorithms (Genetic Algorithm (GA), Simulated Annealing (SA), and Tabu Search (SA)) are executed on the scheduling problem, and the best-performing solution is automatically selected.
[201] Automated Clash Detection: The fitness function systematically identifies and penalizes lecturer clashes, room clashes, capacity violations, and NLP constraint violations.
[202] Web-Based Interface: A Streamlit-powered user interface provides role-based access for administrators, lecturers, and students with real-time timetable visualization.
[203] RESTful API Backend: A FastAPI backend enables programmatic access to the scheduling engine, supporting dataset upload, constraint parsing, timetable generation, and retrieval.

[205] 3.4.1 System Overview
[206] The proposed system, named "OptimaSched" (also referred to as "Time4Tables" in the user interface), is a multi-layered intelligent timetable scheduling platform. The system accepts academic resource datasets in CSV format, parses natural language constraints from administrators, generates an initial feasible timetable, and then applies three competing optimization algorithms to produce an optimized, clash-minimized schedule.

[498] 3.12 Algorithm Design
[499] 3.12.1 Overview of the Hyper-Heuristic Approach
[500] The system employs a selection hyper-heuristic framework, which operates at a higher level of abstraction than individual meta-heuristics. Rather than applying a single optimization algorithm, the system executes three distinct meta-heuristic algorithms on the same scheduling problem and selects the best-performing solution based on fitness evaluation (Burke et al., 2013; Drake et al., 2020).
[501] The three algorithms implemented are:
[502] Genetic Algorithm (GA) — a population-based evolutionary algorithm
[503] Simulated Annealing (SA) — a trajectory-based probabilistic algorithm
[504] Tabu Search (TS) — a memory-based neighbourhood search algorithm
[505] 3.12.2 Problem Representation
[506] Each timetable solution is represented as a list of dictionaries, where each dictionary represents a single scheduled session. This representation is consistent across all three algorithms and the fitness function.
[507] The search space is defined by the Cartesian product of available timeslots and venues. With 6 days × 10 hours = 60 timeslots and approximately 30+ venues, each course can potentially be assigned to any of 60 × 30 = 1,800 (day, hour, venue) combinations, making the total search space exponentially large.

[571] 3.14 Fitness Function Design
[572] 3.14.1 Mathematical Formulation
[573] The fitness function evaluates the quality of a timetable solution by computing a weighted sum of constraint violations. A lower fitness value indicates a better (more feasible) solution. The fitness function is defined as:
[574] $$F(T) = w_1 \cdot V_{LC} + w_2 \cdot V_{RC} + w_3 \cdot V_{CV} + w_4 \cdot V_{PV} + w_5 \cdot V_{US} + w_6 \cdot V_{NLP}$$ Where:
[575] Symbol
[576] Description
[577] Weight ($w_i$)
[578] $V_{LC}$
[579] Number of lecturer clashes
[580] $w_1 = 100,000$
[581] $V_{RC}$
[582] Number of room clashes
[583] $w_2 = 100,000$
[584] $V_{CV}$
[585] Number of capacity violations
[586] $w_3 = 50,000$
[587] $V_{PV}$
[588] Number of practical violations
[589] $w_4 = 10,000$
[590] $V_{US}$
[591] Number of unscheduled sessions
[592] $w_5 = 500,000$
[593] $V_{NLP}$
[594] Number of NLP constraint violations
[595] $w_6 = 200,000$
[596] 3.14.2 Violation Detection Logic
[597] 1. Lecturer Clash Detection ($V_{LC}$)
[598] The Math: $V_{LC} = \sum \sum \max(0, count - 1)$
[599] What it means: We loop through every single timeslot and every single lecturer. If a lecturer is teaching 1 class (or zero), everything is fine. But if they are accidentally double-booked and teaching 2 classes at once, we take 2 - 1 = 1 and add 1 point to the Lecturer Clash penalty score.
[600] 2. Room Clash Detection ($V_{RC}$)
[601] The Math: $V_{RC} = \sum \sum \max(0, count - 1)$
[602] What it means: Just like the lecturers, we check every timeslot and every classroom. If there are 2 different courses scheduled in the exact same room at the exact same time, we subtract 1 to find the overlap, and add that overlap to the Room Clash penalty score.
[603] 3. Capacity Violation Detection ($V_{CV}$)
[604] The Math: $V_{CV} = \sum \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } S_i > C_i \ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$
[605] What it means: We look at every scheduled class session. If the number of enrolled students ($S_i$) is greater than the room's maximum seating capacity ($C_i$), we add 1 to the Capacity Violation penalty score. If the room is big enough, we add 0.

[608] 3.14.4 Weight Justification
[609] The penalty weights are hierarchically assigned based on the severity of each constraint violation:
[610] Unscheduled Sessions ($w_5 = 500,000$): The highest penalty because an unscheduled course represents a complete failure to allocate a required academic activity.
[611] NLP Violations ($w_6 = 200,000$): High penalty to ensure that administrator-specified constraints are respected.
[612] Lecturer and Room Clashes ($w_1 = w_2 = 100,000$): Equal high penalties because both types of clashes render the timetable physically infeasible.
[613] Capacity Violations ($w_3 = 50,000$): Moderate penalty because while overcrowding is undesirable, it does not prevent the class from occurring.
[614] Practical Violations ($w_4 = 10,000$): Lowest penalty for violations related to practical/laboratory session requirements.

[658] 3.16.3 Genetic Operators
[659] Tournament Selection:
[660] Tournament selection works by randomly sampling a subset of the population (tournament size = 5) and selecting the individual with the lowest (best) fitness value. This method provides good selection pressure while maintaining diversity.
[661] Single-Point Crossover:
[662] The crossover operator creates a child solution by combining genetic material from two parent solutions. A random crossover point is selected, and the child inherits sessions from parent 1 before the point and from parent 2 after the point.
[663] Mutation:
[664] The mutation operator modifies individual sessions within a timetable by reassigning their timeslot and venue. Each session has a 20% probability of being mutated. The mutation includes a recovery mechanism that attempts to reschedule any sessions that become unscheduled during mutation.

[668] 3.17 Simulated Annealing Methodology
[669] 3.17.1 Algorithm Overview
[670] Simulated Annealing (SA) is a probabilistic meta-heuristic optimization algorithm inspired by the annealing process in metallurgy, where materials are heated and then slowly cooled to reduce defects (Delahaye et al., 2019). In the context of timetable scheduling, SA explores the solution space by iteratively generating neighbouring solutions through mutation and accepting or rejecting them based on the Metropolis acceptance criterion.

[693] 3.17.3 Metropolis Acceptance Criterion
[694] The core of SA is the acceptance probability function, which determines whether a neighbouring solution is accepted:
[695] $$P(\text{accept}) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } \Delta E < 0 \text{ (improvement)} \ e^{-\Delta E / T} & \text{if } \Delta E \geq 0 \text{ (deterioration)} \end{cases}$$ Where:
[696] $\Delta E = f(\text{neighbour}) - f(\text{current})$ is the change in fitness
[697] $T$ is the current temperature
[698] At high temperatures, the algorithm accepts deteriorating moves with high probability (encouraging exploration). As the temperature decreases, the acceptance probability for worse solutions approaches zero (encouraging exploitation).
[699] 3.17.4 Cooling Schedule
[700] The system uses a geometric cooling schedule:
[701] $$T_{k+1} = \alpha \cdot T_k$$
[702] where $\alpha = 0.995$ is the cooling rate. This ensures a gradual transition from exploration to exploitation over the course of the optimization.

[708] Tabu Search (TS) is a meta-heuristic optimization algorithm that uses an adaptive memory structure (the tabu list) to guide the search away from previously visited solutions, thereby avoiding cycling and encouraging exploration of new regions of the search space (Sörensen et al., 2018). [751] The mutation operator is a critical component shared by all three algorithms. It generates neighbouring solutions by randomly modifying the timeslot and venue assignments of individual sessions. The mutation includes a two-phase approach:
[752] Phase 1 — Random Reassignment:
[753] Each session has a configurable probability (mutation rate) of being selected for reassignment.
[754] Selected sessions are removed from their current timeslot/room and assigned to a new valid timeslot/room combination that does not conflict with existing assignments.
[755] Phase 2 — Recovery Scheduling:
[756] Any sessions that become unscheduled during Phase 1 undergo a recovery attempt, where the algorithm tries all available timeslot/room combinations to find a feasible assignment.

[1166] 4.7 Performance Analysis
[1167] 4.7.1 Why the Genetic Algorithm Performed Best
[1168] The Genetic Algorithm emerged as the best-performing algorithm for several reasons:
[1169] Population Diversity: Unlike SA and TS which maintain a single current solution, GA maintains a population of 50 diverse solutions. This population-level diversity enables more thorough exploration of the search space, increasing the probability of finding high-quality solutions.
[1170] Crossover Operator: The single-point crossover combines beneficial scheduling patterns from two different parent solutions, potentially creating children that inherit good timeslot/room assignments from both parents. This recombination capability is absent in SA and TS.
[1171] Elitism: The preservation of the top 5 individuals across generations ensures that the bestfound solution is never lost, providing a monotonically non-increasing best fitness trajectory.
[1172] Tournament Selection Pressure: Tournament selection with a size of 5 provides sufficient selection pressure to drive the population toward better solutions while maintaining enough diversity to avoid premature convergence.
[1173] Balanced Mutation Rate: The 20% mutation rate strikes an effective balance between exploration (modifying existing solutions) and exploitation (preserving good assignments).
[1174] 4.7.2 Why Simulated Annealing Underperformed
[1175] Simulated Annealing's relative underperformance can be attributed to:
[1176] Single-Solution Trajectory: SA maintains only one current solution, limiting its ability to explore distant regions of the search space simultaneously.
[1177] Acceptance of Deteriorating Moves: While the Metropolis criterion enables escape from local optima, it can also lead the search away from promising regions, particularly at high temperatures.
[1178] Parameter Sensitivity: The algorithm's performance is sensitive to the initial temperature and cooling rate. The chosen parameters ($T_0 = 10,000$, $\alpha = 0.995$) may not be optimal for this specific problem instance.
[1179] No Recombination: Unlike GA, SA has no mechanism for combining good components from different solutions.
[1180] 4.7.3 Tabu Search Performance Assessment
[1181] Tabu Search performed better than SA but slightly worse than GA:
[1182] Effective Cycling Prevention: The tabu list successfully prevented the search from revisiting previously explored solutions, ensuring continuous exploration.
[1183] Neighbourhood Diversity: The higher mutation rate (0.3) and multiple neighbour generation (20 per iteration) provided good exploration diversity.
[1184] Memory Overhead: The tabu list's signature comparison introduces computational overhead, contributing to the longer execution time.
[1185] Limited by Neighbourhood Size: With only 20 neighbours per iteration, the algorithm's exploration is constrained compared to GA's population of 50 individuals.

[1396] 4.11 Discussion
[1397] 4.11.1 Effectiveness of the Hyper-Heuristic Approach
[1398] The hyper-heuristic framework proved effective in this study by allowing multiple algorithms to compete on the same problem instance and automatically selecting the best-performing solution. This approach addresses a fundamental limitation of single-algorithm systems — namely, the uncertainty about which algorithm will perform best for a given problem instance (Burke et al., 2013). By running all three algorithms and comparing their results, the system guarantees that the administrator receives the best available solution.
[1399] 4.11.2 Impact of NLP Integration
[1400] The integration of Natural Language Processing for constraint specification significantly improved the system's usability compared to traditional form-based or code-based constraint definition. The regex-based NLP parser successfully translated common scheduling constraints from English text to machine-readable formats. However, the current implementation is limited to a predefined vocabulary of constraint patterns and does not support free-form natural language understanding.
[1401] 4.11.3 Scalability Considerations
[1402] The system demonstrated adequate performance for the Covenant University dataset (490+ courses,
[1403] 200+ lecturers, 50+ venues). However, for larger institutions with thousands of courses, the computational cost of the Genetic Algorithm (which evaluates 50 individuals per generation, each requiring a full fitness evaluation) may become a bottleneck. Potential optimizations include parallel fitness evaluation, incremental fitness updates, and adaptive population sizing.
[1404] 4.11.4 Comparison with Related Work
[1405] The results obtained in this study align with findings in the literature:
[1406] Burke et al. (2013) demonstrated that hyper-heuristic approaches outperform singlealgorithm solutions for timetabling problems, a finding consistent with our comparative results.
[1407] Ceschia et al. (2023) established the NP-hard nature of timetable scheduling, justifying the use of meta-heuristic approaches rather than exact algorithms.
[1408] Delahaye et al. (2019) demonstrated SA's effectiveness for combinatorial optimization, though our results show GA outperforming SA for this specific problem instance.
[1409] Sörensen et al. (2018) documented Tabu Search as an effective memory-based metaheuristic, which our results confirm through TS's superior performance over SA.
[1410] 4.12 Summary
[1411] This chapter presented the implementation details, user interface design, experimental setup, and comprehensive results of the hyper-heuristic timetable generation system. The Genetic Algorithm achieved the best overall performance with a fitness score of 150,000 (94.12% improvement over the initial solution), followed by Tabu Search (250,000, 90.20% improvement) and Simulated Annealing (350,000, 86.27% improvement). All three algorithms successfully scheduled all courses without any unscheduled sessions and eliminated or significantly reduced lecturer and room clashes. The NLP constraint parser correctly extracted all tested constraint patterns, demonstrating the feasibility of integrating natural language reasoning with hyper-heuristic optimization for university timetable generation.

[1416] 5.2 Summary
[1417] 5.2.1 Problem Statement
[1418] The university timetable scheduling problem (UTSP) is a well-documented NP-hard combinatorial optimization problem that requires assigning courses to timeslots and venues while satisfying multiple hard and soft constraints. Traditional manual and semi-automated approaches are timeconsuming, error-prone, and incapable of producing optimized schedules for large academic institutions.
[1419] 5.2.2 Research Objectives Revisited
[1420] This study set out to:
[1421] Design and implement an intelligent timetable generation system that integrates natural language processing with hyper-heuristic optimization algorithms.
[1422] Develop a Natural Language Processing (NLP) module that enables administrators to specify scheduling constraints in plain English.
[1423] Implement three meta-heuristic optimization algorithms — Genetic Algorithm, Simulated Annealing, and Tabu Search — as competing heuristics within a hyper-heuristic framework.
[1424] Evaluate and compare the performance of the three algorithms using standardized metrics.
[1425] Develop a user-friendly web-based interface for timetable generation, visualization, and management.
[1426] 5.2.3 Methodology Summary
[1427] The study adopted the Design Science Research (DSR) methodology with an experimental approach. The system was developed using Python 3.10+ with Streamlit (frontend), FastAPI (backend), and SQLite (database). The Covenant University academic dataset (490+ courses, 200+ lecturers, 50+ venues) was used as the primary test dataset. Three optimization algorithms were implemented and compared using a weighted fitness function that penalizes lecturer clashes, room clashes, capacity violations, unscheduled sessions, and NLP constraint violations.

[1489] 5.4 Findings
[1490] The major findings from this study are:
[1491] Finding 1: Genetic Algorithm Superiority for UTSP
[1492] The Genetic Algorithm consistently outperformed Simulated Annealing and Tabu Search in terms of fitness score optimization. This superiority is attributed to:
[1493] Population-level diversity enabling broader search space exploration
[1494] Crossover operator combining beneficial scheduling patterns from multiple solutions
[1495] Elitism preserving the best solutions across generations
[1496] Finding 2: All Algorithms Achieve Significant Improvement
[1497] All three algorithms achieved substantial improvements over the initial greedy heuristic solution (86 –94% fitness reduction), confirming the effectiveness of meta-heuristic optimization for the university timetable scheduling problem.
[1498] Finding 3: Trade-off Between Solution Quality and Execution Time
[1499] A clear trade-off exists between solution quality and computational cost:
[1500] GA: Best quality (150,000) but moderate execution time (~45s)
[1501] SA: Fastest execution (~30s) but lowest quality (350,000)
[1502] TS: Longest execution (~55s) with intermediate quality (250,000)
[1503] Finding 4: NLP Integration Enhances Usability
[1504] The NLP constraint parser significantly simplifies the constraint specification process compared to traditional form-based or code-based approaches. Administrators can express institutional policies in plain English without requiring technical knowledge of the underlying algorithm parameters.
[1505] Finding 5: Capacity Violations Are the Most Persistent
[1506] While lecturer and room clashes were effectively eliminated by GA and TS, capacity violations persisted across all algorithms (3 violations each). This finding suggests that capacity constraints are structurally harder to resolve when venue capacity distributions do not align with student enrolment patterns.

[1516] 5.6 Limitation
[1517] Despite the achievements of this study, the following limitations are acknowledged:
[1518] NLP Scope: The NLP constraint parser is based on rule-based regex pattern matching and is limited to a predefined vocabulary. It does not support complex or ambiguous natural language inputs. Advanced NLP techniques such as transformer-based language models (e.g., BERT, GPT) were not employed.
[1519] Single-Semester Scope: The system generates timetables for a single semester and does not account for multi-semester planning or year-long scheduling considerations.
[1520] No Multi-Objective Optimization: The fitness function combines all objectives into a single weighted sum. True multi-objective optimization (e.g., Pareto-based approaches) was not implemented.
[1521] Static Dataset: The system operates on a static CSV dataset and does not support real-time data updates or integration with a live student information system.
[1522] Limited Constraint Types: The current implementation does not support all possible constraint types (e.g., consecutive-hour blocking for multi-hour courses, department-specific room preferences, break-time enforcement).
[1523] Parameter Tuning: The algorithm parameters (population size, mutation rate, cooling rate, tabu list size) were set based on common literature values and preliminary testing, not through systematic hyperparameter optimization.
[1524] Single-Institution Testing: The system was tested only on the Covenant University dataset and may require adaptation for institutions with significantly different scheduling structures.
[1525] Fragmented and Partially Estimated Dataset: A significant data collection limitation of this study is that the academic dataset used for system testing was not sourced from a single integrated university information system. Instead, data was gathered from multiple independent sources, including the Directorate of Academic Planning Unit (DAPU), timetable officers across individual departments, and direct departmental records. This multi-source collection process introduced several data quality challenges:
[1526] (i) Inconsistent or incomplete lecturer assignment records across departments.
[1527] (ii) Missing or ambiguous venue capacity information, which in several instances required estimation based on physical inspection and informed judgement rather than official records.
[1528] (iii) Lack of standardized data formats across departments, requiring extensive manual cleaning and normalization before the dataset could be used as system input.
[1529] As a result, the generated timetable, while algorithmically optimal given the available data, may not perfectly reflect true institutional scheduling constraints. The accuracy of the optimized schedule is therefore bounded by the accuracy and completeness of the input dataset.
[1530] 5.7 Conclusion
[1531] This study successfully designed, implemented, and evaluated a hyper-heuristic university timetable generation system that integrates Natural Language Processing with three meta-heuristic optimization algorithms — Genetic Algorithm, Simulated Annealing, and Tabu Search. The system demonstrates that:
[1532] Natural language reasoning can be effectively integrated with meta-heuristic optimization to create user-friendly scheduling systems that bridge the gap between nontechnical administrators and complex algorithmic optimization.
[1533] The hyper-heuristic approach of running multiple algorithms and selecting the bestperforming solution is a robust strategy for timetable scheduling, as the relative performance of algorithms may vary across problem instances.
[1534] The Genetic Algorithm is the most effective meta-heuristic for the university timetabling problem in this context, achieving a 94.12% fitness improvement with complete elimination of lecturer and room clashes.
[1535] All three meta-heuristic algorithms significantly outperform the initial greedy heuristic, confirming the value of optimization-based approaches for NP-hard scheduling problems.
[1536] The modular system architecture supports easy extension with additional algorithms, constraint types, and interface features, making it a flexible platform for future timetabling research.
[1537] The system represents a significant step toward automated, intelligent university timetable generation that respects both hard institutional constraints and soft stakeholder preferences expressed in natural language.
[1538] 5.8 Recommendations
[1539] Based on the findings and limitations of this study, the following recommendations are made:
[1540] 5.8.1 For Institutional Adoption
[1541] Pilot Deployment: It is recommended that the system be piloted at the departmental level before full institutional deployment, allowing administrators to familiarize themselves with the interface and validate the generated timetables against known scheduling requirements.
[1542] Data Quality Assurance: Institutions should establish standardized course registration data formats and invest in data quality processes to ensure that the input dataset accurately reflects current course offerings, lecturer assignments, and venue capacities.
[1543] Training: Academic administrators should receive brief training on the system's NLP constraint specification capabilities to maximize the effectiveness of the natural language interface.
[1544] 5.8.2 For Future Development
[1545] Advanced NLP Integration: Future versions should incorporate transformer-based language models (e.g., GPT-4, BERT) for more robust and flexible natural language constraint parsing, supporting complex, ambiguous, and contextual constraint specifications.
[1546] Multi-Objective Optimization: Implement Pareto-based multi-objective optimization to simultaneously optimize multiple competing objectives without requiring predefined weight assignments.
[1547] Real-Time Data Integration: Connect the system to institutional student information systems (SIS) and learning management systems (LMS) for real-time data access and automatic schedule updates.
[1548] Cloud Deployment: Deploy the system on cloud infrastructure (e.g., AWS, Google Cloud) to support multi-institution usage, horizontal scaling, and collaborative scheduling.
[1549] Additional Algorithms: Extend the hyper-heuristic framework with additional metaheuristics such as Ant Colony Optimization (ACO), Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), and Harmony Search.
[1550] 5.9 Future Work
[1551] 5.9 Future Work
[1552] The following areas represent promising directions for future research:
[1553] Deep Learning-Based Constraint Learning: Develop machine learning models that automatically learn institutional scheduling patterns from historical timetables, reducing the need for explicit constraint specification.
[1554] Hybrid Algorithm Design: Investigate hybrid approaches that combine the strengths of multiple algorithms (e.g., GA-SA hybrid, GA-TS hybrid) within a single optimization framework.
[1555] Dynamic Rescheduling: Develop algorithms for dynamic timetable adjustment in response to real-time events such as lecturer absences, room closures, or emergency schedule changes.
[1556] Exam Timetabling Extension: Extend the system to support examination scheduling, which introduces additional constraints such as student exam spacing, invigilator assignment, and conflict-free exam periods.
[1557] Mobile Application: Develop a companion mobile application for lecturers and students to view their personalized schedules, receive notifications about schedule changes, and provide feedback.
[1558] Reinforcement Learning: Explore the application of reinforcement learning for adaptive algorithm selection in the hyper-heuristic framework, where the system learns which algorithm performs best for different types of scheduling problems over time.
[1559] Constraint Satisfaction Problem (CSP) Integration: Integrate CSP solvers as an additional layer of constraint verification before meta-heuristic optimization, ensuring hard constraint feasibility at every stage.
[1560] Institutional Data Integration and Synchronisation: Future work should prioritise the development of a centralised institutional data pipeline that draws directly from a unified University Management Information System (UMIS). Rather than relying on data collected from fragmented sources such as individual departmental timetable officers and the Directorate of Academic Planning Unit (DAPU), future implementations should:
[1561] (i) Integrate with the university's official student registration system to obtain verified course enrolment figures.
[1562] (ii) Connect to a verified venue capacity database maintained by the university's facilities management unit, eliminating the need for estimated capacity values.
[1563] (iii) Establish an automated data synchronisation mechanism that updates course, lecturer, and venue records in real time, ensuring that the timetable generation system always operates on the most current and accurate institutional data.
[1564] Such an integrated data foundation would substantially improve the accuracy, fairness, and institutional validity of the generated timetables, and would represent a critical step toward full production-readiness for institutions of any scale.
